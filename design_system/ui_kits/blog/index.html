The kernel limit is a fixed point, not a target.

I've been writing "the network converges to the NTK" for years. I now think this is the wrong sentence — and the right one is more interesting.

Why I keep getting this wrong

If you've read more than two papers about wide networks, you've seen something like this: "in the infinite-width limit, training reduces to kernel regression with the NTK". It's a true sentence. But the way I — and I think a lot of us — read it the first time is subtly off.

The natural reading is asymptotic: the network is trying to be a kernel machine, and the wider you make it, the closer it gets. Wrong direction. The network isn't approaching the kernel limit. The kernel limit is the regime in which the network's features stop moving — a fixed point of the feature-learning dynamics, not a goal it aspires to.1

The picture

Here is the diagram I should have drawn for myself five years ago. The features $\phi_\theta(x)$ evolve under gradient flow:

$$ \frac{d\phi_\theta(x)}{dt} = - \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} \cdot \nabla_\theta \phi_\theta(x). $$

In the NTK regime, $\nabla_\theta \phi_\theta(x)$ is approximately constant in $\theta$ — the features do not move. That's the whole story. Not "the network resembles a kernel machine" but "the features have stopped learning".

The interesting question isn't "when does the network look like the kernel limit?" — it's "when does it leave?".

A short proof

The textbook derivation goes through second-order Taylor expansion of the loss. Here's a shorter one. Let $K_t = \nabla_\theta f_t \nabla_\theta f_t^\top$ be the empirical NTK at training step $t$. Then:

# in the kernel regime, K is constant in t
assert torch.allclose(K(theta_t), K(theta_0), atol=1e-3)
# equivalently, the feature gradient barely moves
assert torch.allclose(grad_phi(theta_t), grad_phi(theta_0), atol=1e-3)

So the regime is defined by the constancy of $K$, not its value. Once we say it that way, the obvious next question — under what training dynamics does $K_t$ vary? — is where all of feature learning lives.

What it doesn't mean

It doesn't mean kernel methods are uninteresting. The kernel limit is still the cleanest thing we know how to analyse. It means we should stop using "approaches the NTK" as shorthand for "wide networks are simple". They aren't simple. They are linearised, which is a much more specific and useful thing to say.

Notes
  1. This framing is not original to me. It comes out of a long line of work — see Yang & Hu (2021) on µP, and Bordelon & Pehlevan (2022) on self-consistent dynamics. I'm just trying to write the version I wish I'd read first.
