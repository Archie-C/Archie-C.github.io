topic 03 · subtopic 3.2 · jan 30

Momentum, Nesterov, and what they actually do.

Gradient descent walks downhill. Momentum walks downhill with a memory of where it has been — and that small change buys an entire order in the convergence rate.

The update, written out

Heavy-ball momentum keeps a running velocity $v_t$ and uses it in place of the bare gradient:

$$ v_{t+1} = \beta\, v_t + \nabla f(x_t), \qquad x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta\, v_{t+1}. $$

With $\beta = 0$ you get plain gradient descent. With $\beta$ close to one, the iterate behaves like a damped particle rolling on the loss surface — which is exactly the right mental picture.

Convergence, before and after

For an $L$-smooth, $\mu$-strongly-convex $f$, plain gradient descent converges at rate $(1 - \mu/L)^T$. Nesterov's accelerated method, with the right schedule, achieves $(1 - \sqrt{\mu/L})^T$ — the square-root in the exponent is the win, and it is optimal for first-order methods on this class.

exercise · 3.2.1 warm-up

Take $f(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(x_1^2 + 100\, x_2^2)$. Plot the trajectories of plain GD and heavy-ball momentum for $\beta \in \{0, 0.5, 0.9\}$. Where does each method first cross within $10^{-3}$ of the optimum?

Implementation, end to end

def momentum_gd(grad_f, x0, eta=1e-2, beta=0.9, T=2000):
    x, v = x0, np.zeros_like(x0)
    for _ in range(T):
        v = beta * v + grad_f(x)
        x = x - eta * v
    return x

Six lines. The interesting bit is choosing $\beta$ — too small and you lose the acceleration, too large and the velocity overshoots and you oscillate. Most practical schedulers warm $\beta$ up over training, which we'll come back to in 3.4.
