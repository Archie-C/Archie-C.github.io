Regularisation and the Bias-Variance Tradeoff.

What if our model is too powerful?

What this note covers

  • Why we need regularisation
  • Types of regularisation
  • The bias-variance tradeoff

For a more formal definition, see Deep learning: Foundations and Concepts

Why

In the previous section we introduced non-linear regression, which can be very powerful but can also be too complex relative to the task. As a result, we may produce a model that fits the training data very well but performs poorly on unseen data. We call this overfitting.

It's a topic that will come up repeatedly in this course, and in machine learning more broadly as it is one of the central challenges we need to address when building a model. We need to try to fit the data as well as possible, but still make sure we aren't just fitting noise or memorising the training data. Our model must be able to generalise to new, unseen data.

Don't be like Dave

To illustrate this, let's look at an example. So my friend (let's call him Dave) wants to invest in the stock market and he thinks he can predict the future prices of a stock using the past price movements. He collects data on the stock price and decides to fit a linear regression model (which is actually quite common in finance) with past prices as inputs and the future prices as outputs. He finds that it doesn't quite perform as well as he'd hoped, so he decides to add in some basis functions. He adds in polynomial features up to degree 10 and suddenly his model fits the training data perfectly. He decides that he's cracked the code and invests all his money based on the predictions of this model. Unfortunately, the predictions are terrible and he loses it all. What went wrong? Dave's model was too powerful and it overfit the training data. It was able to fit the noise in the data rather than the underlying signal, and as a result it failed to generalise to the new movements.

How to not be like Dave

We still want to use a powerful model, but just constrain it in some way so that it doesn't overfit the training data. This is where regularisation comes in.

On a high level, what we do is add a penalty term to our loss function to discourage overly complex models. The penalty term penalises large weights in the model, encouraging small weights and better generalisation. The two most common types of regularisation in regression are L1 regularisation (also known as Lasso) and L2 regularisation (also known as Ridge).

L1 Regularisation (Lasso)

L1 regularisation uses the L1 norm of the weight vector as a penalty term. The L1 norm is just the sum of the absolute values of the weights.

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{L1}} = \mathcal{L} + \lambda \sum_{i=1}^n |w_i| $$

We can tune the hyperparameter $\lambda$ to control the strength of the regularisation. For this, there is no closed-form solution, so we need to use a numerical optimisation method.

L2 Regularisation (Ridge)

L2 regularisation uses the L2 norm of the weight vector as a penalty term. The L2 norm is just the sum of the squares of the weights.

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{L2}} = \mathcal{L} + \lambda \sum_{i=1}^n w_i^2 $$

Again, we can tune the hyperparameter $\lambda$ to control the strength of the regularisation. There is a closed-form solution for this case. It is given by:

$$ \mathbf{\theta}_{\text{L2}} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X} + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y} $$

where $\mathbf{I}$ is the identity matrix. This also has a nice property that it guarantees a unique solution even when $\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X}$ is not invertible, which can happen when we have more features than data points.

The bias-variance tradeoff

In the same spirit as regularisation, we often have to make a tradeoff between how well our model fits the training data and how well it generalises to unseen data. This is the bias-variance tradeoff.

Let's say the data is generated by some function $f(x)$ and we have some noise $\epsilon$ that is added to the output. So our data points are generated by the following process:

$$ y = f(x) + \epsilon ,\quad \mathbb{E}[\epsilon] = 0 ,\quad \text{Var}[\epsilon] = \sigma^2 $$

We train a model $\hat{f}(x)$ using dataset $D$, which means that $\hat{f}(x)$ is a random variable that depends on the dataset $D$ that we used to train it. So therefore, if we care about the expected error of our model on some new data generated by the same process, we need to take the expectation over both the dataset and the noise:

$$ \mathbb{E}_{D, \epsilon}[(y - \hat{f}(x))^2] = \mathbb{E}_{D}[(f(x) - \hat{f}(x))^2] + \sigma^2 $$

Essentially, what we are doing here is taking the average error of our model across:

  • All different possible datasets
  • All different realisations of the noise

This instantly tells us something about the expected error. One part is unavoidable. The noise in the generation process.

The other part is the error due to our model not being able to capture the true function $f(x)$ perfectly. We can further decompose the error into two parts: bias and variance. To do that we first need to define a new quantity. The mean prediction across datasets:

$$ \bar{f}(x) = \mathbb{E}_D[\hat{f}(x)] $$

Then we can have the following decomposition. We can say that the deviation of our model from the average model is $\bar{f}(x) - \hat{f}(x)$. And then the deviation of the average model from the true function is $f(x) - \bar{f}(x)$. So we can write:

$$ \mathbb{E}_{D}[(f(x) - \hat{f}(x))^2] = \mathbb{E}_{D}[((f(x) - \bar{f}(x)) + (\bar{f}(x) - \hat{f}(x)))^2] $$

It seems counter intuitive at first that we are adding and subtracting the same quantity, but once you expand this expression you are left with:

$$ \mathbb{E}_{D}[(f(x) - \hat{f}(x))^2] = (f(x) - \bar{f}(x))^2 + \mathbb{E}_{D}[(\bar{f}(x) - \hat{f}(x))^2] $$

We can say the first term represents the bias squared (the deviation of the average model from the true function), and the second term represents the variance (the deviation of individual models from the average model). So overall we have the following decomposition:

$$ \mathbb{E}_{D, \epsilon}[(y - \hat{f}(x))^2] = \text{bias}^2 + \text{variance} + \sigma^2 $$

What does this mean?

When you train a model you typically only see one dataset. But there are many datasets you could have seen. So imagine training your model many times on those different datasets.

At a high level, if you take the average of all of those models you get the average model $\bar{f}(x)$. The bias is the error of this average model from the true function. So it essentially tells you if your model is systematically wrong in some way. For example, you are trying to fit a linear model to data that is generated by a quadratic function. In this case, the bias will be large because the average model is a straight line and the true function is a curve.

On the other hand, the variance is a measure of the instability of your model across the different datasets. Essentially it is the error of your model from the average. So if your model is very sensitive to the specific dataset you trained it on, then the variance will be large. In the example of the linear model, the variance will also be large as it will be sensitive to the specific data points it sees.

The aim of the machine learning researcher is to find a model that has both a low bias and a low variance, which is often a tradeoff.

An example

Lets look at some data that we know the generation process for (in practice you may have an idea or none at all but rarely will you know exactly). And lets take our linear regression model - with and without basis functions - and try to fit it to the data.

Below we have some data generated by a cubic function with some noise added to it. We then fit 3 regression models to this data:

  • Cubic regression (with $x^3$ basis functions)

  • Ninethic regression (with $x^9$ basis functions)

First the data on its own, you can see it is quite noisy but there is a clear cubic curve.

Linear regression — high bias, low variance

A linear model can never capture a cubic curve — no matter how much data it sees, the straight line is systematically wrong. This is high bias. However, the two fits below look very similar to each other even though they were trained on slightly different datasets. That stability is low variance.

fig 2–3 The same linear model fitted to two slightly different draws from the same process. The lines are consistent across datasets (low variance) but consistently miss the curvature (high bias).

Cubic regression — correct complexity

Now we use a cubic polynomial — the right model class for this data. Because the true generator is also cubic, the model can in principle recover the exact function. Both fits are good and remain consistent across the two datasets: low bias and low variance.

fig 4–5 Cubic fits on both datasets. The model matches the true functional form and gives consistent, accurate predictions across draws.

9th degree regression — low bias, high variance

Finally, a 9th degree polynomial. With 10 free parameters and only 9 data points, the model can interpolate all training points exactly — training error is essentially zero. But look at how drastically different the two fitted curves are. A small change in the data leads to a completely different model. This is high variance. Dave's model.

fig 6–7 9th degree fits on both datasets. The model passes through all training points but varies wildly between datasets — a hallmark of high variance and severe overfitting. Values beyond ±300 are clipped for readability.

key takeaway

The right model complexity minimises the sum of bias squared and variance. Regularisation is one tool that lets you trade a small increase in bias for a significant reduction in variance — often a very worthwhile exchange.
